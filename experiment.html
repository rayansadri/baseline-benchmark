01

Problem Statement

The current finance review process relies on three sources of input: structured questionnaires, uploaded documents, and a short human-led interview. Each has known weaknesses that compound across cases.

Questionnaires produce predictable, rehearsed answers. The same phrasing appears across applications because applicants understand what reviewers want to hear. Uploaded documents are frequently incomplete or missing entirely. When they are present, they show figures without context — no explanation of origin, no classification of debt, no clarity on whether collateral is a gift or a loan.

The interview, when it happens, is the most valuable input of the three. But its quality depends entirely on who is running it, how much time they have, and whether they ask the right follow-up questions. The result is inconsistency. One reviewer probes funding sources with precision; another accepts "my family will help" as a complete answer.

Reviewers frequently begin cases without the evidence they need. Monthly payment plans are stated without math. Motivation answers are generic. Work history details are listed without depth. Contradictions between what was written and what was said go unnoticed.

The goal of this experiment is not to automate the final decision. It is to improve the quality of inputs available to the reviewer before that decision is made. Better inputs lead to better decisions, more consistent approvals, and cases that can be audited and explained.

02

Solution Statement

The solution is a student-friendly voice interview layer that sits upstream of the review process.

ElevenLabs powers the voice interface. It speaks the questions, listens to the student's answers, and generates a transcript. That transcript is then passed to Claude or a backend processing layer, which structures the raw answers into reviewer-ready notes: Program Fit observations, Financial Resources observations, contradiction flags, and missing information markers.

The human reviewer reads the structured output alongside the raw transcript and makes the final decision.

This separation is intentional. No single layer is trying to do too much. Each does one thing well and passes the result to the next.

03

Product Thinking

The voice interview is designed around a set of principles that keep the experience useful, honest, and easy to maintain.

  • One question at a time. Compound questions produce compound confusion. Each question is a single, clear ask. No multi-part prompts.
  • Simple English. The interview is designed to be accessible to applicants at all language levels. Technical vocabulary is avoided unless essential to the question.
  • Warm and approachable. The tone is conversational, not interrogative. The student should feel they are being listened to, not processed.
  • At most one follow-up per topic. The agent can ask a single clarifying question if the first answer is vague. It does not loop or drill. One pass per topic.
  • No fake automation theater. The student is not pretending to talk to a human and neither is the system. The experience is direct and honest.
  • Same downstream outputs, better input layer. Nothing in the review workflow changes. The same output categories exist. The same human reviewer makes decisions. Only the quality of inputs improves.
  • Human in the loop. At every stage — design, testing, review, and decision — there is a human involved. The system supports human judgment; it does not replace it.
04

Current Scope

The current experiment is scoped to two fixed output categories:

  • Program Fit — covering motivation, program understanding, past study relevance, and work relevance to the program field.
  • Financial Resources — covering funding source and origin, existing debt and its nature, cash collateral classification (gift or loan with repayment terms), monthly payment capacity, income stability during study, and backup plan if primary income stops.

These two were selected because they represent the areas where current input collection is most consistently weak, and where better evidence most directly affects reviewer confidence and decision quality.

The voice layer is designed to capture stronger, more specific evidence for these two outputs. It does not replace any existing step in the review process. Additional output categories may be added in future scope if this experiment succeeds.

The flow begins when a student opens the interview link. ElevenLabs starts the voice agent and runs the interview from start to finish. When the interview ends, ElevenLabs generates a transcript and sends it via webhook to the backend. The backend stores the transcript alongside case metadata.

Claude then processes the stored transcript into four structured output types: Program Fit notes, Financial Resources notes, contradiction flags, and a list of missing or unanswered information. The human reviewer opens the case, reads the structured output alongside the raw transcript if needed, and makes the final approval decision.

The critical design choice in this architecture is where intelligence is applied. ElevenLabs is treated as a recording layer. Claude is the reasoning layer. The webhook is the connection between them. The reviewer is the decision layer. None of these steps overlaps with another.

06

What This Experiment Tests

This experiment tests one specific question: does a structured voice interview produce higher quality inputs for finance review than the current process?

Higher quality is defined precisely:

  • More specific answers — less vague, less rehearsed, harder to bluff
  • Better financial clarity — funding source classified, amounts stated, gift vs. loan confirmed
  • More consistent across cases — the same questions, the same structure, every time
  • Faster reviewer readiness — the structured output reduces the time a reviewer needs before making a confident decision

The benchmark is not whether the AI sounds smart. It is not whether the conversation feels natural or whether the voice is pleasant. The benchmark is whether the resulting structured output gives the reviewer clearly better material than what they have today.

If a reviewer, looking at the structured Claude output from this interview, can make a more confident decision with less effort, the experiment has succeeded.

07

Current Interview Logic

The interview is organized into three sequential parts. Each is focused on a specific type of evidence and feeds a specific downstream output category.

Part 1 — Program Fit

Covers the applicant's motivation for choosing the program, their understanding of what it involves, their past academic background and its relevance, and their work history and its connection to the field. The goal is to separate specific, verifiable knowledge from rehearsed, generic answers.

  • Genuine motivation — not polished but hollow
  • Specific program knowledge — named courses, named outcomes
  • Academic preparation — depth, not just field alignment
  • Professional relevance — actual tasks and outputs, not job titles

Part 2 — Consistency Check

A small set of bridging questions that revisit key claims from Part 1 using different phrasing. This is not a second interview — it is a brief check designed to surface vague or contradictory answers before entering the financial section, while the context of Part 1 is still fresh.

  • Confirming stated career goals with specifics
  • Cross-checking time frames and claimed experience
  • Identifying inconsistencies before they carry into financial answers

Part 3 — Financial Resources

Covers the applicant's funding source and origin, existing debt and its nature, classification of cash collateral as gift or loan (with repayment terms where applicable), monthly payment capacity, income stability during study, and backup plan if the primary income source stops.

  • Gift vs. loan — must be explicitly classified, not left open
  • Specific dollar amounts — not ranges, not approximations
  • Realistic monthly math — income, expenses, and buffer confirmed
  • Named backup plan — a real named fallback, not "I'll find something"
08

Key Learnings So Far

  1. Overbuilding inside ElevenLabs was the wrong direction. Early experiments tried to implement branching logic, dynamic follow-ups, and scoring directly inside the voice agent. This produced inconsistent behavior, was difficult to debug, and broke frequently under real conditions.
  2. ElevenLabs should stay as the input layer only. The voice agent speaks questions and captures answers. All routing, logic, and analysis happens after the transcript is produced. The moment you ask ElevenLabs to do reasoning, you lose reliability.
  3. The real intelligence belongs after transcript creation. The Claude or backend processing layer is where reasoning should happen. Transcript in, structured output out — this is the right architecture. Anything else creates fragile dependencies.
  4. Long voice questions break easily. Questions longer than two sentences become awkward when spoken aloud and harder for applicants to hold in mind. Short, direct questions produce cleaner, more useful answers.
  5. Binary financial questions produce cleaner answers. "Is this a gift or a loan?" returns a classifiable answer. "Can you describe your funding arrangement?" returns a paragraph that still needs to be parsed. Force the classification in the question.
  6. A hard stop at the end of the interview matters. Without a clean, definitive closing line, the agent behavior after the final question is unpredictable. The interview must end clearly. Any ambiguity at the close creates problems.
  7. The opportunity is better evidence capture, not full automation. This experiment is not about removing the human reviewer. It is about giving them better material before they open a case. The goal is consistency and completeness, not replacement.
09

Known Issues Observed in Early Tests

  • Some questions still sound too interrogative when spoken aloud. Phrasing that reads cleanly on screen can feel like pressure when voiced by the agent.
  • Long questions occasionally trigger awkward repetition behavior in the voice layer. Questions with more than two clauses are consistently problematic.
  • Debt answers can remain fuzzy even when asked directly. The follow-up question on debt classification needs tighter phrasing — the current version still allows vague answers to pass.
  • Monthly payment plan answers are easy to bluff. Stating intent ("I'll work part-time") rather than named figures and confirmed arrangements is still being accepted without a follow-up challenge.
  • End behavior requires a hard, unambiguous closing line. Any softness in the closing — any phrasing that leaves the door open — causes the agent to attempt to continue after the final question.
10

Benchmarking and Stress Test Approach

The system will be stress tested using ten synthetic student personas, each representing a distinct answer profile. The purpose is to compare transcript quality and downstream Claude output quality across different combinations of program fit and financial strength.

Each test will produce a raw transcript, a Claude-structured output, and a qualitative assessment of reviewer readiness. Tests will be run in sequence and results compared across profiles.

Recurring failure modes in specific question types will be documented and used to refine the question set once before moving toward real applicant testing. The goal is to identify the minimum set of questions that reliably produces high-quality reviewer inputs across the widest range of applicant profiles — including the hardest ones.

14

Why This Approach Matters

This experiment does not try to replace human reviewers. The decision remains with the reviewer, always.

The experiment addresses a specific and tractable problem: reviewers are being asked to make important decisions with incomplete, inconsistent, and hard-to-audit inputs. That is not a reviewer failure. It is a system design failure. The inputs are weak because the collection process is weak.

Better inputs lead to better decisions, faster reviews, and a record of what was collected and when. Cases become easier to defend, explain, and learn from. Consistency across reviewers improves because the input layer is standardized rather than dependent on individual interview style or available time.

The ambition here is not artificial intelligence replacing judgment. It is systematic evidence collection making judgment easier, more consistent, and better. A reviewer who can make a confident decision in less time, with less uncertainty, is a better outcome for everyone — including the applicant.

15

Next Steps

  1. Run 10 structured interview tests using synthetic personas T01 through T10 in sequence.
  2. Collect all 10 transcripts and pass each through the Claude analysis layer to generate structured outputs.
  3. Compare outputs across profiles — identify recurring failure modes in specific question types, especially in financial classification and backup plan questions.
  4. Document weak spots — record the exact questions and answer patterns that produce vague, unclassifiable, or missing outputs.
  5. Refine the question set once based on observed evidence, not assumptions. One targeted revision, not a redesign.
  6. Validate the refined version with 3–5 additional test runs covering the highest-risk profiles.
  7. Define transcript quality success criteria before moving to any real applicant testing. The threshold must be written down and agreed on.
  8. Move toward backend automation of the transcript processing step once transcript quality is proven stable and consistent across profiles.
