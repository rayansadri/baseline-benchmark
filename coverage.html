Program Fit
A — Trait Definition
Measures how well the applicant's prior education, work experience, and stated motivation align with the specific program they are applying to — including whether their expectations are realistic and their reasons for choosing the program are genuine.

C — Missing or Weak Areas
Gap 1
No structured test of program knowledge
Realistic understanding (50% of SOP score) is assessed from whatever the applicant volunteers. There is no question that directly tests whether they researched the program. Misinformed applicants score 3 by default if they sound confident.
Gap 2
Part-time hours never confirmed
The Duration sub-score is systematically over-scored because hours per week are almost never explicitly confirmed. Resumes show date ranges only. The adjustment rule exists but has no input to act on.
Gap 3
Volunteer work detection is passive
The rule (volunteer = lower quality) is correct, but detection depends entirely on the officer catching professional-sounding volunteer language in the resume or interview. No probe exists to surface this distinction.
Gap 4
Motivation signal is a single one-shot answer
The entire 50% motivation score comes from one open-ended question. Generic answers are common. There is no follow-up logic to push past surface-level reasons or test whether the applicant has real conviction versus just saying the right words.
Gap 5
Depth of knowledge is inferred, not demonstrated
Education Fit and Industry Experience both have a Depth sub-score (30% weight). But depth is inferred from course titles and job titles — not from the applicant actually demonstrating they understand the work they claim to have done.

Financial Resources
A — Trait Definition
Assesses whether the applicant has a realistic, verifiable financial position to support loan repayment — including their assets, debt, how they are sourcing the upfront cash collateral, and (for onshore applicants) their current income versus expenses.
B — Current Subdimensions Covered Today
Subdimension Coverage Status Current Input Source How Assessed Today Signal Quality Comments
Assets (general) Covered but weak Loan questionnaire, interview transcript Officer extracts declared assets from both sources and cross-verifies. Flags discrepancies over CAD 5,000 side-by-side. Medium Rule and cross-check exist. Weakness: inputs depend entirely on what the applicant voluntarily reports. No structured asset-by-category enumeration. Applicants often bundle figures ("I have some savings") without itemizing.
Canadian vs. back-home asset split Barely covered Loan questionnaire, interview transcript Rubric instructs officer to separate CA assets (higher weight) from back-home assets (lower weight). No structured question enforces this split in the interview. Weak The weighting rule is correctly defined but cannot be applied accurately because applicants frequently report a bundled savings figure. The CA vs. back-home distinction is almost never explicitly asked. Officers often guess or skip the split.
Liquidity ordering Covered but weak Loan questionnaire, interview transcript Rubric defines liquidity order: Savings > Car > Land > House > Other. Officer applies this hierarchy when weighting assets. Medium Hierarchy is clear and correct. Weakness: it can only be applied if assets are itemized. If the applicant gives a single bundled figure, the hierarchy has nothing to act on. Quality of input determines whether the rule fires at all.
Debt (declared) Covered but weak Loan questionnaire, interview transcript, Canadian credit report (onshore) Officer cross-verifies all three sources. Any mismatch is a high-priority flag. Soft vs. hard lender type recorded. Monthly payment and duration evaluated. Medium Cross-verification rule is strong and correctly prioritized. Weakness: almost every applicant says "no debt" in the interview. The rule exists to catch contradictions, but if no contradiction is visible on the surface, undeclared debt is not probed.
Source of cash collateral Covered but weak Loan questionnaire, interview transcript Officer identifies the source (gift, loan, asset sale) and cross-verifies between questionnaire and interview. Mismatch is a high-priority flag. Weak Classification logic is correct and important. Weakness: the question is typically one open-ended ask. Vague answers like "my family will help" are extremely common and cannot be accurately classified as gift vs. loan without a follow-up.
Gift vs. loan vs. asset sale Barely covered Loan questionnaire, interview transcript Rubric defines clear rules: gift adds to assets, loan adds to debt, sale is neutral. Officer applies rules after identifying the source type. Weak The downstream rule is airtight. The upstream detection is the failure point. "My parents are helping me" is genuinely ambiguous — it could be a gift or a soft loan. Without a direct clarifying question ("will you pay it back?"), classification is a guess.
Onshore income and expenses Covered well Interview transcript, questionnaire, bank documents Full income statement required for onshore-auto applicants. Monthly income vs. expenses evaluated. CAD 1,000/mo threshold applied. Relocation risk checked. Strong Most complete section in the financial resources rubric. Logic is well-structured for onshore applicants. Main gap: not available at all for offshore applicants, leaving a significant blind spot for their cash flow picture.
Relocation risk Barely covered Interview transcript (implied) Rubric notes: if the job is in a different city from the school, income may be lost when repayment starts. No structured question exists to surface this. Weak The risk is correctly identified in the rubric. But the input to detect it (job city vs. school city) is almost never explicitly confirmed in the interview. Officers must infer from context or remember to ask. Frequently missed.
Questionnaire vs. interview contradictions Covered but weak Loan questionnaire vs. interview transcript Rubric instructs officer to compare both sources and flag discrepancies side-by-side. Debt mismatches are highest priority. Asset mismatches over CAD 5,000 are flagged. Medium Rules are correctly designed and prioritized. Weakness: the officer notices contradictions only if they are obvious from a side-by-side read. There is no real-time contradiction probe during the interview to surface inconsistencies while the applicant can still respond.
Repayment realism Barely covered Interview transcript (one question) Summary section includes a "Monthly Payment Plan" field. Officer records how the applicant plans to make payments. If answer is absent or unreasonable, field reads "No answer provided." Weak One of the most important signals for actual repayment risk, and the weakest in the current system. Vague answers ("I'll find work") are accepted. There is no follow-up to test feasibility. No monthly math is done. No comparison to expenses.
C — Missing or Weak Areas
Gap 1
Collateral source classification is guesswork
"My family will help" cannot be classified as gift vs. loan without a direct question. This single ambiguity changes whether the amount goes into Total Assets or Total Debt — a material difference in the score.
Gap 2
Canadian vs. back-home asset split almost never confirmed
The CA asset weighting rule is one of the most important rules in the rubric for onshore applicants. But the split is rarely asked explicitly. Officers often apply the higher weight to figures they cannot verify are actually in Canada.
Gap 3
Undeclared debt is not probed
Cross-verify exists for declared debt. But there is no structured follow-up when an applicant says "no debt." Credit cards, informal family loans, and soft debt are routinely undisclosed unless explicitly asked.
Gap 4
Repayment realism has no math behind it
The monthly payment plan field accepts any answer. "I'll work part-time" is recorded and treated the same as a specific, calculated plan. No follow-up exists to test whether the stated plan actually covers the monthly obligation.
Gap 5
Relocation risk is almost never surfaced
Job city vs. school city is not a standard question. For onshore applicants who rely on current income for repayment, this is a high-impact risk factor that is frequently missed entirely.
