Program Fit & Financial Resources

Working view — two traits only. Back to full baseline →

1. Program Fit

Assesses how well the applicant's background — work history, education, and stated goals — aligns with the specific program they have applied to.

External dependency — rubric not inline The Program Fit rubric is not hardcoded in this baseline. It is injected at runtime via {% reference program_fit %}. The content varies per program and must be supplied by the system before assessment begins. If this reference is empty or fails to resolve, score Program Fit as N/A and request the program rubric under Extra Documents.

What to assess

Despite the external rubric, the assessment approach follows the same pattern as all other scored categories. Use the injected rubric to evaluate:

  • Whether prior work experience aligns with the program's typical applicant profile
  • Whether the applicant's education provides relevant foundational knowledge
  • Whether the stated motivation is consistent with what the program actually offers
  • Whether the applicant's career goals are achievable via this specific program

Output format

Score on a 1–5 scale using the injected rubric criteria. Format:

Program Fit (4): The applicant's three years in healthcare administration directly maps to the program's core intake profile. Minor gap in technical certification requirements.
If rubric is unavailable Mark as N/A. In the written note, state: "Program Fit rubric was not available at time of assessment. Score cannot be assigned." Add the rubric to document_requests.

2. Financial Resources

Assesses the applicant's complete financial picture: assets, debt, and cash collateral sourcing. Logic expands for onshore-auto applicants who have Canadian financial data available.

Onshore Auto — additional data available The following Canadian data may be available and should be incorporated: monthly employment income, rent and living expenses, verified Canadian bank balances, and a Canadian credit report (utilization + repayment history). Canadian-based assets carry more weight than assets back home.
Standard — offshore applicants Assets and debt assessed from loan questionnaire and interview transcript only. No Canadian financial records available. Income statement section does not apply.

2.1 Assets

Cross-verification first. Compare declared assets in the questionnaire against the interview transcript. Place discrepancies side-by-side.

Example: "Mentioned CAD 14,000 in savings in the questionnaire but said CAD 17,000 in the interview transcript."

Assets in Canada — higher weight
  • Canadian savings (with account verification if available)
  • Car registered in Canada
  • Other liquid assets — GICs, chequing accounts

For onshore applicants, these are prioritized over assets back home.

Assets back home — lower weight
  • Savings
  • Land
  • Car
  • House
  • Other assets (investments, businesses)
Liquidity order (highest → lowest):
Savings → Car → Land → House → Other Assets

2.2 Source of Cash Collateral

Cash collateral is the upfront amount the student provides to secure the loan — typically CAD 4,500 to CAD 18,000. How it is sourced materially affects the total asset and debt picture.

Source type Effect on assets Effect on debt
Gift from friends or family +Added as additional asset No change
Loan from friends, family, or lender No change +Added to total debt
Asset sale (liquidation) Shifts illiquid → liquid; no net change No change
Total Assets = Reported Assets + Gift Collateral (if gifted)
Total Debt = Reported Debt + Loan Collateral (if borrowed)

2.3 Debt Evaluation

Cross-verification first. Compare questionnaire, interview transcript, and credit report (onshore only) side-by-side.

Example: "Mentioned CAD 4,000 in debt in the questionnaire but credit report shows CAD 6,200 total balance."

FactorNotes
Lender typeSoft (family/friends) vs. Hard (bank/institution)
Loan amountAll debts combined, including cash collateral loan
Monthly repaymentCurrent payment obligation
Repayment durationHow long the obligation lasts
Credit utilization rateFrom credit report — onshore only
Repayment historyMissed or late payments — onshore only
High-priority flag Debt value mismatches between the questionnaire and interview transcript are high importance discrepancies. Always escalate. Do not treat them as minor.

2.4 Income Statement (Onshore Auto only)

Evaluate the student's short-term cash flow by analyzing current income and monthly expenses.

FactorNotes
Employment status Employed or not. If earning >CAD 1,000/month and job appears serious → treat as stable income.
Monthly income Exact figure. Flag if discrepancy between questionnaire and interview.
Relocation risk If the job is in a different city from the school, the student may lose income when repayment starts.
Rent Current monthly rent
Other expenses Utilities, food, transportation, etc.

2.5 Full Financial Picture — Scoring Guidance

The final Financial Resources score accounts for all of the above together. Score on a 1–5 scale.

ScoreProfile
5 Strong liquid assets (verified Canadian savings), low or no debt, collateral sourced via gift or own savings, stable income well above expenses (onshore).
4 Decent assets with minor debt. Collateral source is clear. Income covers expenses with some margin (onshore).
3 Moderate assets but some debt. Collateral partially borrowed. Income roughly meets expenses. Verifiability concerns present.
2 Low liquid assets, significant debt, collateral fully borrowed, or income falls short of expenses.
1 Very weak financial position. Assets are minimal or unverifiable, heavy debt load, no clear collateral plan, or major discrepancies across sources.

Key weighting principles:

  • Liquidity and accessibility of Canadian-based assets carry the most weight (onshore)
  • Debt structure and repayment behavior is weighted higher than raw asset size
  • Unverifiable or inconsistent figures should push the score down
  • A gifted collateral is a positive signal; borrowed collateral is a risk factor
Output format Financial Resources (3): Applicant reports CAD 8,000 in savings (questionnaire) vs. CAD 10,000 (interview) — minor discrepancy. Collateral of CAD 6,000 is a soft loan from parents, increasing total debt to CAD 6,000. No Canadian credit history. Income of CAD 1,200/month is slightly above expenses, but relocation to Toronto for school removes that buffer.
